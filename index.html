01 · PhD Research (Ongoing) · NTU Taiwan · Feb 2026 – Present

Optical Metrology, AI Super-Resolution & Semiconductor Inspection

At the Precision Metrology Lab in NTU's Graduate School of Advanced Technology, my doctoral research addresses one of the most pressing challenges in modern semiconductor manufacturing: measuring structures that are smaller than the wavelength of light used to inspect them. As transistor architectures shrink into the sub-nanometer regime for technologies like Gate-All-Around FETs (GAAFET) and 3D NAND memory, the metrology infrastructure must advance faster than the devices themselves — or yield improvement becomes impossible.

01
Optical Critical Dimension (OCD) Metrology

Developing scatterometry-based methods where reflected light from periodic nanostructures encodes their 3D geometry. I build inverse-problem solvers using Rigorous Coupled-Wave Analysis (RCWA) to extract critical dimension, height, and sidewall angle with picometer-level precision — entirely non-destructive.

02
AI-Driven Super-Resolution Imaging

Integrating deep learning architectures with physics-based optical forward models to push image resolution beyond the classical diffraction limit — combining the interpretability of physics with the power of data-driven reconstruction methods.

03
Instrumentation & System Integration

Designing interferometric optical setups incorporating broadband light sources, high-speed detectors, and precision translation stages for automated wafer-level inspection — bridging algorithmic advances with physical measurement systems.

04
Nanomaterials & Device Characterization

Applying optical and electrical characterization to thin films and nanomaterials for device performance evaluation, with applications in optoelectronics and sensor calibration.

02 · Master's Thesis · Jadavpur University · 2024–2025

Lead-Free High-Entropy Perovskite Oxide Nanomaterials

The global push to eliminate toxic lead from functional materials has opened a fascinating frontier in perovskite science. For my master's thesis, I synthesized a novel class of lead-free, high-entropy perovskite oxide — a material where five or more metal cations are simultaneously incorporated into a single crystallographic phase. This configurational entropy, rather than conventional stoichiometric control, becomes the primary stabilizing force, enabling entirely new elemental combinations that would otherwise phase-separate and decompose.

The synthesis was carried out via the sol-gel route, chosen for its ability to achieve atomic-level homogeneity at low processing temperatures and relatively low cost — a key consideration if these materials are ever to translate from laboratory curiosity into scalable device fabrication. The process involved precise chelation of metal precursors, controlled hydrolysis, and a carefully designed annealing protocol to drive phase formation without entropy collapse.

Structural and functional characterization formed the heart of this work. X-ray diffraction with Rietveld refinement confirmed single-phase formation and enabled quantitative extraction of lattice parameters and site occupancies. FESEM with EDS mapping verified spatial homogeneity of all five cation species. UV-Vis spectroscopy yielded the optical bandgap, while FTIR confirmed metal-oxygen bonding environments. TGA and DSC provided a thermodynamic window into the material's thermal stability and the entropic contribution to phase stabilization.

The results point toward a compelling dual application: the measured thermoelectric response makes this material a candidate for energy conversion devices, while the engineered electronic structure opens a pathway toward photocatalytic hydrogen evolution — directly relevant to green energy research and the growing effort to replace lead-based perovskites in next-generation electronics.

Student Guide — For Indian Students

I went from a regular college in Paschim Medinipur to a PhD seat at National Taiwan University — without clearing IELTS, without a famous undergraduate institution, and without knowing anyone abroad. This guide is everything I wish someone had told me when I was starting out.

India is at an inflection point in its semiconductor journey. With the India Semiconductor Mission channelling billions into fabrication plants in Gujarat and Assam — backed by companies like Tata Electronics, Micron, and CG Power — the demand for trained engineers who understand both the physics and the process is growing faster than the supply. This is not a trend; it is a structural shift that will define the next two decades of Indian high technology.

What makes semiconductor engineering particularly valuable is the depth of foundation it demands. You cannot fake knowledge of solid-state physics, device operation, or thin-film processing. This high entry barrier is also your shield — once you have built genuine expertise, you occupy a position that is extremely difficult to automate or offshore. A Physics or Electronics graduate from any Indian university who commits seriously to this field has a legitimate path to global opportunities, because the knowledge itself is portable even when the institution name is not.

The cold email is the single most underused tool available to Indian students trying to reach foreign labs. Most students either never send one, or send a generic template that no professor will remember. The difference between an email that gets a reply and one that gets deleted in three seconds is specificity — and specificity requires genuinely reading the professor's work before you write a single word.

Your email should open by naming a specific paper of theirs, explaining what in that paper connected with your own work or thinking, and then concisely describing what you have done that is relevant to their research direction. Keep the entire email under 200 words. Attach a one-page CV and nothing else unless they ask. Do not write "Respected Sir/Madam" — use their proper title and last name. Send individual, personalized emails, never mass mail. Follow up once after two weeks if there is no reply.

Your CV is not a list of everything you have done — it is a curated argument for why a particular professor should invest time in you. The single most important entry, by a large margin, is your thesis or research project. Describe it in two to three lines that are specific about what you did, what technique you used, and what result you obtained. Vague lines like "studied nanomaterials" tell a professor nothing.

Skills matter only when they are honest and specific. List tools you can actually use — MATLAB, LaTeX, Origin, Python, specific characterization equipment — and nothing else. Keep the CV to one page for most applications. Use LaTeX to typeset it — this signals to a professor in a technical field that you are someone who takes precision seriously.

Start with the research question, not the university ranking. Identify two or three topics you are genuinely interested in, then use Google Scholar to find the most active researchers publishing in those areas in the last three years. Activity in the recent past matters more than historical prestige — a professor who published consistently in 2023 and 2024 is likely running a funded, active lab with room for students.

Once you have a shortlist, read their group website carefully. Look for whether they have open positions, what funding sources they list, and whether their current students' work overlaps with yours. Reaching out to current students is invaluable — they will tell you things the professor's webpage never will.

Many research-focused graduate programs, particularly in Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, and parts of Europe, either waive the English language test requirement or accept alternatives that Indian students can easily satisfy. In Taiwan specifically, NTU and NTHU waive IELTS and TOEFL if your undergraduate or graduate education was conducted entirely in English — which is true for almost every Indian student. You typically need a medium-of-instruction letter from your university's examination office, available at no cost.

For programs that require a score, check whether they accept the Duolingo English Test, which is significantly cheaper and faster to schedule. Some programs also waive the requirement entirely through specific fellowship pathways — the TEEP program in Taiwan is one such route where research ability matters far more than standardised test scores.

Taiwan's Taiwan Experience Education Program (TEEP) is, in my view, the most accessible and well-structured short-term research internship available to Indian students in semiconductor and materials science. It is fully funded — including flight, accommodation, and a monthly stipend — and places you in a Taiwanese university laboratory for three to six months. I did my TEEP at NTHU's College of Semiconductor Research in 2025, and it led directly to my PhD position at NTU.

Within India, the INUP workshops at IITs and IISc are excellent for gaining hands-on cleanroom and characterization experience. The IISc winter school at CeNSE is particularly rigorous — performing well there is something worth noting prominently on your CV. These domestic programs are often overlooked by students focused on going abroad, but they are precisely what makes your profile competitive enough to get there.

In a physics or electronics research career, the tools you use to process, analyze, and present data are nearly as important as the experiments themselves. MATLAB is the lingua franca of signal processing, optical simulation, and numerical methods — knowing it well enough to write your own scripts is a meaningful differentiator. Python with NumPy, SciPy, and Matplotlib is equally essential, especially if you intend to work with AI or data-driven methods.

Origin is the standard tool for scientific data plotting and curve fitting in materials science. Learn it properly, not just basic plotting. LaTeX is non-negotiable for anyone who intends to write papers, theses, or research proposals. Professors notice immediately when a student submits a well-typeset document — it signals care and precision. Start learning it during your M.Sc., not when the thesis deadline arrives.
