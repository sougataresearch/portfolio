01 · Master's Thesis · Jadavpur University

Lead-Free High-Entropy Perovskite Oxide Nanomaterials

The global push to eliminate toxic lead from functional materials has opened a fascinating frontier in perovskite science. For my master's thesis, I synthesized a novel class of lead-free, high-entropy perovskite oxide — a material where five or more metal cations are simultaneously incorporated into a single crystallographic phase. This configurational entropy, rather than conventional stoichiometric control, becomes the primary stabilizing force, enabling entirely new combinations of elements that would otherwise phase-separate.

The synthesis was carried out via the sol-gel route, chosen for its ability to achieve atomic-level homogeneity at low processing temperatures and relatively low cost — a key consideration if these materials are to translate from the laboratory into scalable device fabrication. The sol-gel process involved precise chelation of metal precursors, controlled hydrolysis, and a carefully designed annealing protocol to drive phase formation without entropy collapse.

Structural and functional characterization formed the heart of this work. X-ray diffraction with Rietveld refinement confirmed single-phase formation and allowed quantitative extraction of lattice parameters, site occupancies, and strain. FESEM with EDS mapping verified the spatial homogeneity of all five cation species across the microstructure. UV-Vis spectroscopy was used to extract the optical bandgap, while FTIR confirmed the nature of metal-oxygen bonding environments. Finally, TGA and DSC provided a thermodynamic window into the material's thermal stability and the entropic contribution to phase stabilization — the very quantity that defines a high-entropy system.

The results point toward a compelling dual application: the measured Seebeck coefficient and low thermal conductivity make this material a candidate for thermoelectric energy conversion, while the engineered electronic structure opens a pathway toward photocatalytic hydrogen evolution — directly relevant to clean energy research. This work contributes to the growing understanding of how entropy engineering can be used to design functional oxides without relying on the periodic table's heavier, more hazardous elements.

02 · PhD Research (Ongoing) · NTU Taiwan

Optical Metrology, AI Super-Resolution & Semiconductor Inspection

At the Precision Metrology Lab in NTU's Graduate School of Advanced Technology, my doctoral research addresses one of the most critical bottlenecks in modern semiconductor manufacturing: the ability to measure structures that are smaller than the wavelength of light used to inspect them. As transistor dimensions shrink into the sub-nanometer regime for technologies like Gate-All-Around FETs (GAAFET) and 3D NAND memory, the metrology tools must evolve faster than the devices themselves.

01
Optical Critical Dimension (OCD) Metrology

Developing scatterometry-based methods where reflected light from periodic nanostructures encodes their geometry. I build inverse-problem solvers using Rigorous Coupled-Wave Analysis (RCWA) to extract critical dimension, height, and sidewall angle with picometer-level precision — without destroying the wafer.

02
AI-Driven Super-Resolution Imaging

Integrating deep learning architectures with physics-based optical forward models to push image resolution beyond the classical diffraction limit. The goal is to reconstruct nanoscale features from diffraction-limited measurements — combining the interpretability of physics with the adaptability of data-driven methods.

03
Instrumentation & System Integration

Designing and optimizing interferometric optical setups incorporating broadband light sources, high-speed detectors, and precision translation stages for automated wafer-level inspection — bridging algorithmic advances with physical measurement systems.

04
Nanomaterials & Device Characterization

Applying optical and electrical characterization techniques to thin films and nanomaterials for device performance evaluation, with applications in optoelectronics and sensor calibration — connecting materials understanding with metrological outcomes.

Student Guide — For Indian Students

I went from a regular college in Paschim Medinipur to a PhD seat at National Taiwan University — without clearing IELTS, without a famous undergraduate institution, and without knowing anyone abroad. This guide is everything I wish someone had told me when I was starting out.

India is at an inflection point in its semiconductor journey. With the India Semiconductor Mission channelling billions into fabrication plants in Gujarat and Assam — backed by companies like Tata Electronics, Micron, and CG Power — the demand for trained engineers who understand both the physics and the process is growing faster than the supply. This is not a trend; it is a structural shift that will define the next two decades of Indian high technology.

What makes semiconductor engineering particularly valuable is the depth of foundation it demands. You cannot fake knowledge of solid-state physics, device operation, or thin-film processing. This high entry barrier is also your shield — once you have built genuine expertise, you occupy a position that is extremely difficult to automate or offshore. A Physics or Electronics graduate from any Indian university who commits seriously to this field has a legitimate path to global opportunities, because the knowledge itself is portable even when the institution name is not.

The cold email is the single most underused tool available to Indian students trying to reach foreign labs. Most students either never send one, or send a generic template that no professor will remember. The difference between an email that gets a reply and one that gets deleted in three seconds is specificity — and specificity requires genuine reading of the professor's work before you write a single word.

Your email should open by naming a specific paper of theirs, explaining what in that paper connected with your own work or thinking, and then concisely describing what you have done that is relevant to their research direction. Keep the entire email under 200 words. Attach a one-page CV and nothing else unless they ask. Do not call them "Respected Sir/Madam" — use their proper title and last name. Send individual, personalized emails, never mass mail. Follow up once after two weeks if there is no reply. The professors who respond are often the ones who are actively looking — your job is simply to be specific enough that they notice you.

Your CV is not a list of everything you have done — it is a curated argument for why a particular professor should invest time in you. The single most important thing on it, by a large margin, is your thesis or research project. Describe it in two to three bullet points that are specific about what you did, what technique you used, and what result you obtained. Vague lines like "studied nanomaterials" tell a professor nothing and waste their time.

Skills matter only when they are honest and specific. List tools you can actually use — MATLAB, LaTeX, Origin, Python, specific characterization equipment — and nothing else. Certifications from Coursera are worth including if they are from reputable universities and relevant to the field you are applying to. Keep the CV to one page for most applications unless you genuinely have publications that justify a second. Use LaTeX to typeset it — this alone signals to a professor in a technical field that you are someone who takes precision seriously.

Start with the research question, not the university ranking. Identify two or three topics you are genuinely interested in, then use Google Scholar to find the most active researchers publishing in those areas in the last three years. Activity in the recent past matters more than historical prestige — a professor who published consistently in 2023 and 2024 is likely running a funded, active lab that has room for students. A famous name who has not published in five years may have shifted focus or reduced lab size.

Once you have a shortlist, read their group website carefully. Look for whether they have open positions stated, what funding sources they list, and whether their current students' work overlaps with yours. LinkedIn can also reveal where their alumni have gone — this tells you a great deal about the kind of mentorship and career outcomes the lab produces. Reach out to current students if you can; they will tell you things the professor's webpage never will.

Many research-focused graduate programs, particularly in Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, and parts of Europe, either waive the English language test requirement or accept alternatives that Indian students can satisfy without a formal exam. In Taiwan specifically, a large number of universities — including NTU and NTHU — will waive IELTS and TOEFL if your undergraduate or graduate education was conducted entirely in English, which is true for almost every Indian student. You typically need a letter from your institution confirming the medium of instruction, which your university office can provide.

For programs that require a score, check whether they accept Duolingo English Test, which is significantly cheaper and faster to schedule than IELTS or TOEFL. Some programs also waive the requirement entirely if you are applying through a specific fellowship or internship pathway — the TEEP program in Taiwan, for example, is one such route where research ability matters far more than standardised test scores. Always read the admission requirements carefully for the specific program, not just the university's general page.

Taiwan's Taiwan Experience Education Program (TEEP) is, in my view, the most accessible and well-structured short-term research internship available to Indian students in semiconductor and materials science. It is fully funded — including flight, accommodation, and a monthly stipend — and places you in a Taiwanese university laboratory for three to six months. The application is open, competitive, and evaluated primarily on your research background and the match with a host professor's work. I did my TEEP at NTHU's College of Semiconductor Research in 2025, and it directly led to my PhD position at NTU.

Within India, the INUP (Indian Nanoelectronics Users Programme) workshops at IITs and IISc are excellent for gaining hands-on cleanroom and characterization experience that you would otherwise only get at a well-funded foreign lab. Applying to these schools early — even as an M.Sc. student — builds both skills and a demonstrable track record. The IISc winter school at CeNSE is particularly rigorous; scoring well there is something worth noting on your CV. These domestic programs are often overlooked by students focused on going abroad, but they are precisely what makes your profile competitive enough to get there.

In a physics or electronics research career, the tools you use to process, analyze, and present data are nearly as important as the experiments themselves. MATLAB is the lingua franca of signal processing, optical simulation, and numerical methods in the kind of research I do — knowing it well enough to write your own scripts, not just run existing ones, is a meaningful differentiator. Python with NumPy, SciPy, and Matplotlib has become equally essential, especially if you intend to work with AI or data-driven methods at any point.

Origin is the standard tool for scientific data plotting and fitting in materials science and characterization — if your data from XRD or UV-Vis goes anywhere, it likely goes into Origin. Learn it properly, including curve fitting and statistical analysis, not just basic plotting. LaTeX is non-negotiable for anyone who intends to write papers, theses, or research proposals. Professors in Taiwan, Europe, and the US notice immediately when a student submits a well-typeset document — it signals care, precision, and familiarity with the culture of academic writing. Start learning it during your M.Sc. itself, not when your thesis deadline arrives.
