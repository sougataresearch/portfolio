01 · PhD Research (Ongoing) · NTU Taiwan · Feb 2026 – Present

Optical Metrology, AI Super-Resolution & Semiconductor Inspection

At the Precision Metrology Lab in NTU's Graduate School of Advanced Technology, my doctoral research addresses one of the most pressing challenges in modern semiconductor manufacturing: measuring structures that are smaller than the wavelength of light used to inspect them. As transistor architectures shrink into the sub-nanometer regime for technologies like Gate-All-Around FETs and 3D NAND memory, the metrology infrastructure must advance faster than the devices themselves.

01
Optical Critical Dimension (OCD) Metrology

Scatterometry-based inverse problem solvers using RCWA to extract critical dimension, height, and sidewall angle with picometer-level precision — entirely non-destructive.

02
AI-Driven Super-Resolution Imaging

Deep learning architectures with physics-based optical forward models to push image resolution beyond the classical diffraction limit.

03
Instrumentation & System Integration

Interferometric optical setups with broadband light sources, high-speed detectors, and precision translation stages for automated wafer-level inspection.

04
Nanomaterials & Device Characterization

Optical and electrical characterization of thin films for device performance evaluation in optoelectronics and sensor calibration.

02 · Master's Thesis · Jadavpur University · 2024–2025

Lead-Free High-Entropy Perovskite Oxide Nanomaterials

I synthesized a novel class of lead-free, high-entropy perovskite oxide — five or more metal cations simultaneously incorporated into a single crystallographic phase. Configurational entropy, rather than conventional stoichiometric control, becomes the primary stabilizing force, enabling elemental combinations that would otherwise phase-separate.

Synthesis via the sol-gel route. XRD with Rietveld refinement confirmed single-phase formation. FESEM with EDS mapping verified spatial homogeneity. UV-Vis spectroscopy yielded the optical bandgap. TGA and DSC probed thermodynamic entropic phase stabilization — pointing toward thermoelectric energy conversion and photocatalytic hydrogen evolution.

04Student Guide — For Indian Students

I went from a regular college in Paschim Medinipur to a PhD seat at National Taiwan University — without clearing IELTS, without a famous undergraduate institution, and without knowing anyone abroad. This guide is everything I wish someone had told me when I was starting out.

India is at an inflection point in its semiconductor journey. With the India Semiconductor Mission channelling billions into fabrication plants — backed by Tata Electronics, Micron, and CG Power — the demand for trained engineers who understand both the physics and the process is growing faster than the supply. This is a structural shift that will define the next two decades of Indian high technology.

A Physics or Electronics graduate from any Indian university who commits seriously to this field has a legitimate path to global opportunities, because the knowledge itself is portable even when the institution name is not.

The cold email is the single most underused tool available to Indian students trying to reach foreign labs. The difference between an email that gets a reply and one that gets deleted is specificity — specificity requires genuinely reading the professor's work before you write a single word.

Open by naming a specific paper of theirs, explain what connected with your own work, and concisely describe what you have done that is relevant. Keep the entire email under 200 words. Attach a one-page CV. Do not write "Respected Sir/Madam." Follow up once after two weeks.

Your CV is not a list of everything you have done — it is a curated argument for why a particular professor should invest time in you. The most important entry is your thesis: specific about what you did, what technique you used, what result you obtained.

List only skills you can actually use. Keep the CV to one page. Use LaTeX to typeset it — it signals to a technical professor that you take precision seriously.

Start with the research question, not the university ranking. Use Google Scholar to find active researchers publishing in your area in the last three years. Activity in the recent past matters more than historical prestige.

Read their group website carefully. Look for open positions and funding sources. Reaching out to current students is invaluable — they tell you things the professor's webpage never will.

Many research-focused programs in Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, and parts of Europe waive English test requirements. In Taiwan, NTU and NTHU waive IELTS/TOEFL if your education was conducted in English — true for almost every Indian student. You typically just need a medium-of-instruction letter from your university's examination office, available at no cost.

Taiwan's Taiwan Experience Education Program (TEEP) is the most accessible fully-funded short-term research internship available to Indian students in semiconductor and materials science. Flight, accommodation, and monthly stipend included. My TEEP at NTHU's College of Semiconductor Research in 2025 led directly to my PhD position at NTU.

Within India, INUP workshops at IITs and IISc are excellent for hands-on cleanroom experience. The IISc CeNSE winter school is particularly rigorous — performing well is worth noting prominently on your CV.

The tools you use to process, analyze, and present data are nearly as important as the experiments themselves. MATLAB is the lingua franca of signal processing and optical simulation. Python with NumPy, SciPy, and Matplotlib is equally essential for data-driven methods.

Origin is the standard for scientific data plotting in materials science. LaTeX is non-negotiable for papers, theses, and proposals. Start learning it during your M.Sc., not when the deadline arrives.
